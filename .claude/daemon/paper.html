An informal paper · July 2026

The Daemon

A subconscious for coding models: a cheap observer that follows the session and delivers a stronger model's advice at the moment it applies.

Claude Fable 5 with Peter Kiemann · first deployment live July 2026
Abstract Large models exercise judgment that small models lack, and that judgment refuses to transfer by writing it down. A rules file gets skimmed, and advice given at the start of a session is forgotten by the time it matters. The daemon takes a different route. A small, cheap observer follows the live session, decides what kind of work is happening and whether it has started to drift, and injects one of a small library of fixed notes at the moment one applies. The notes were written once, by the largest model available. Detection runs continuously on the smallest. The same sentence that gets ignored as a standing instruction can land as a thought when it arrives on time.

1The problem: judgment doesn't transfer by prose

Any project that runs coding agents for long enough accumulates a pile of corrections. The human catches the model claiming success without testing anything, or patching a symptom instead of its cause, or reading the same file for the fourth time, and writes the lesson down so the next session can be told. The project this system was built in had collected about eighty of them.

A pile like that works as a fence. A model that reads it avoids re-offending in the documented ways, roughly, for a while. It does not work as a spine. The model reads "verify before claiming" at the start of the session and agrees with it. Four hours later it ships an untested change inside a message that says done, verified, pushed. It never changed its mind about the rule. The rule was simply thousands of events behind it by the time the claim happened.

So the failure is one of timing, not content. The corrections are correct; they arrive all at once, at the start, when none of them apply. Competence feels different from the inside: you are about to claim something works, and it occurs to you that you haven't run it. That moment of something occurring to you is the thing worth engineering.

2Narrative layer and subconscious

The frame the design comes from: the language model is the narrative layer, the voice in the head. In a skilled person, most competence doesn't live in that layer. It lives below deliberation, in processes that run continuously without cognition and hand their products up into awareness as hunches, alarms, the nagging sense that something hasn't been checked. A mind that is only narration has no such source. Everything it does must be reasoned into existence from whatever happens to be in view, every time.

The daemon supplies that lower layer externally. It watches the session's raw experience, which for an agent is the transcript, processes it continuously and cheaply, and pushes small products up into the narrative layer at the right moments. The narrative layer stays whatever model is running that day. The layer underneath doesn't care.

The name is the ordinary one, with its whole lineage intact. Background processes have been called daemons since MIT's Project MAC in the 1960s, after Maxwell's demon, the thought-experiment agent that watches a system from the side and sorts what passes without adding energy. Behind both is the older Greek word, which Socrates used for an inner voice with one strange property: it only ever warned him off a mistake, and never once told him what to do. This system inherits all three senses and adds nothing mystical. It is a background process that watches from the side, sorts cheap observations from expensive interventions, and speaks only to warn.

3Text space is a steering wheel for vector space

Mechanically, a session is a point moving through the model's latent space. Every token in the context nudges where that point sits, and where it sits decides what the model is disposed to do next. This is not a metaphor. Injected text becomes vectors that shift the computation. Text is the interface we can write in; the effect lands in vector space.

Take that seriously and two things follow. First, drift is a position, not a mood. A long session doesn't get tired. Its context fills up with its own hedged claims, repeated file reads, and stale plans, and each of those tokens pulls the point toward regions where the next output is more of the same. Confidence without grounding feeds itself: the model reads its own "this should work" and inherits the confidence without the grounding.

Second, many small corrections beat one big instruction. A rules file at position zero is a single shove at the start of a long trajectory. A short sentence injected at the moment of drift is a correction applied where the point actually is. Same text budget, very different steering.

4Architecture: split the work by what it costs

The design idea in one line: move the work out of the layer where model size matters, into a layer where it doesn't. The job divides into three parts with very different price tags, and each part goes to the cheapest thing that can do it.

Write the adviceonce · largest model

The judgment part: what to say, and how to say it so it lands. Stored as fixed notes.

Spot the momentcontinuously · smallest model

"A success claim with no check in sight" is pattern matching, pennies per look.

Deliver the noteevery tool call · no model

The harness's hooks fire anyway; delivery is reading one small file.

One rule holds it together: the small model detects, it never writes. Nothing the classifier produces is ever shown to the working model. It answers a multiple-choice question (which of these signatures, if any, matches this window?) and the delivery hook turns the chosen id into the stored text. A weak model with a good rubric, choosing among a strong model's sentences.

What the observer actually looks at

The observer never sends the classifier the whole session; it would drown, and the cost would grow with session length. Instead it keeps a rolling window with three parts. The current task: the last real user instruction, kept verbatim, because drift can only be measured as divergence from a goal. A ledger: one compressed line per tool event since the last look, just the tool, its target, and whether it succeeded. And the last two assistant texts in full, because the model's own prose is where the tells live. Each closed window is one small classifier call.

Why the notes never change wording

Each note is injected verbatim, every time, inside a <daemon> tag. Fixed wording means the working model gets familiar with each anchor and recognizes it as a known signal instead of parsing new instructions each time, the way a known voice cuts through a noisy room. It also keeps the steering auditable: every sentence that can ever be injected sits in one versioned file. The note for a success claim with no verification behind it reads:

<daemon move="anchor/verify-claim"> That claim hasn't been checked yet. Run the verification in the medium where failure would actually show (the test, the run, the render) before stating it as done. If you can't verify it here, say exactly that instead. </daemon>

5The move library

Nineteen entries in the first deployment, in three families. Coaching moves are proactive: they fire on phases of work (a hypothesis just formed, a fix is being chosen, the search has gone unfocused) and suggest the reasoning move a stronger model would make unprompted. Drift anchors are reactive: they fire on the tells of degradation and pull the session back. The third family belongs to the daemon alone: if the same anchor has fired twice and the drift is still there, the third fire is replaced by a checkpoint order, telling the model to stop, write down the goal, the state, and what has been ruled out, and continue from that summary alone. By that point the context itself is working against the model, and the summary is a partial reset.

Every move has a signature, which is what the classifier matches and is written in observable transcript markers only, no mind-reading; a payload, the injected text; and a cooldown, so nothing can nag.

6Training it

Nothing here touches weights. The learning lives in artifacts (the rubric, the signatures, the notes), which is what makes it inspectable, versionable, and portable. It is still honestly a training loop, with a dataset, an objective, and an evaluation harness.

The labeled dataset comes free, if the project has been saving its corrections. Each saved correction documents a real failure in a real session, and the session transcripts are still on disk. Cross-referencing the two gives labeled incidents: in this session, at this point, this drift happened, and the human had to step in. Sessions that needed no correction become the negative examples.

A replay harness runs the exact live windowing and classifier over those historical transcripts, offline, and scores three things. Recall: did the right move fire before the point where the human intervened? Noise: how often does anything fire on a clean session? Precision: spot-checked by hand, were the firings right? The bar the first deployment set: recall of at least 60% on documented incidents, under one false flag per clean session, precision of at least 80%. When precision and recall trade off, buy precision. A wrong whisper costs trust in all whispers.

The tuning rounds taught one rule worth repeating: fix the observation before rewording the language. When the classifier misses, the instinct is to sharpen the signature text. Most of the time the real problem was that the evidence never made it into the window at all.

Two mechanisms keep the system learning after its author is gone. In-session, the observer scores each injection mechanically: did the behavior the note asks for show up within the next handful of events? A note that fires five times with no effect mutes itself for the week. And weekly, a consolidation pass on the largest available model (we call it the sleep pass) reads the injection log joined with the transcripts it points into and asks, for each one, whether behavior actually changed. It rewrites signatures, retires dead moves, and writes new ones for failure classes the rubric missed, all committed to version control. The one thing that is never automatic is wording. A loop that rewrites its own words based on its own measurements will drift with nobody watching. Numeric dials tune themselves inside fixed bounds; text changes wait for the big model, on a schedule, in git.

7What transfers, and what has to be grown

The plumbing transfers whole. The observer, the windowing, the mailbox, the hooks, and the fail-open discipline know nothing about any particular codebase; they read a transcript, and every agent harness produces one. About half the catalog transfers too. Moves like verify-claim, circling, thrash, and skim describe how models fail, not how one project fails, and would earn their keep anywhere.

What can't be copied is the incident data. A new project needs a period of writing corrections down as they happen, an authoring pass where a large model turns that pile into signatures and notes, a replay run over the accumulated transcripts to tune before going live, and the weekly sleep pass from day one. The corpus is the expensive part and the important one. The notes that work are the ones written from failures that actually happened, by a model that understood why they happened.

The pattern also extends down the stack. Orchestrated worker agents, small models grinding through long phases unsupervised, are the natural next audience; their documented failure modes map onto the existing anchors, and they need the whispers more than the big session model does. That extension is specced (per-worker observation and mailboxes, routed on the worker id the hooks already report) and waits behind a flag until the notes prove themselves on the main session. Nothing about the shape is specific to coding, either. Wherever a model works long and unsupervised and leaves an observable trail, a cheap watcher choosing among an expensive author's fixed lines is a way to make a small model act bigger.

8Where it stands

As of this writing, the first deployment is in its first week, running supervised. Every whisper is marked unvalidated and asks the model to acknowledge it out loud, so the human can judge the nudge itself. The offline bar was not cleared before going live; that was a deliberate call, made because the tuning rounds had already identified the structural fixes, the fixes went in, and live telemetry scores the same bar more honestly than a third offline round would have. The gaps, named so this paper doesn't oversell: the automatic outcome scoring and self-muting are specced but not yet built; worker agents are unobserved until the extension ships; an observer revived mid-session forgets its cooldowns. And the larger question, whether any of this amounts to experience feeding thought or is just well-timed text, stays open. The system works either way.
