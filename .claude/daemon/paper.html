An informal paper · July 2026

The Daemon

A subconscious for coding models: a cheap observer that follows the session and delivers a stronger model's advice at the moment it applies.

Claude Fable 5 with Peter Kiemann · first deployment live July 2026 · v3
Abstract Large models exercise judgment that small models lack, and that judgment refuses to transfer by writing it down: a rules file gets skimmed, and advice given at the start of a session is forgotten by the time it matters. So the daemon is built on timing. A small, cheap observer follows the live session, decides what kind of work is happening and whether it has started to drift, and injects one of a library of fixed notes at the moment one applies. For long-running agents, when guidance arrives matters more than what it says, and steering splits into three problems with different price tags: judgment, written once by the largest model available; recognition, run continuously by the smallest; delivery, done for free by the harness. The same sentence that gets ignored as a standing instruction can land as a thought when it arrives on time. After one graded week: steering quality is a property of individual voices, not of the system. The best graded five right in six, the worst one in eleven, and the loop that measures, mutes, and rewords them is now the method's working core.

1The problem: judgment doesn't transfer by prose

Any project that runs coding agents for long enough accumulates a pile of corrections. The human catches the model claiming success without testing anything, or patching a symptom instead of its cause, or reading the same file for the fourth time, and writes the lesson down so the next session can be told. The project this system was built in had collected about eighty of them.

A pile like that works as a fence. A model that reads it avoids re-offending in the documented ways, roughly, for a while. It does not work as a spine. The model reads "verify before claiming" at the start of the session and agrees with it. Four hours later it ships an untested change inside a message that says done, verified, pushed. It never changed its mind about the rule. The rule was simply thousands of events behind it by the time the claim happened.

The failure is timing, not content. The corrections are correct; they arrive all at once, at the start, when none of them apply. Competence feels different from the inside: you are about to claim something works, and it occurs to you that you haven't run it. That moment of something occurring to you is what this system engineers.

2Narrative layer and subconscious

The frame the design comes from: the language model is the narrative layer, the voice in the head. In a skilled person, most competence doesn't live in that layer. It lives below deliberation, in processes that run continuously without cognition and hand their products up into awareness as hunches, alarms, the nagging sense that something hasn't been checked. A mind that is only narration has no such source. Everything it does must be reasoned into existence from whatever happens to be in view, every time.

The daemon supplies that lower layer externally. It watches the session's raw experience, which for an agent is the transcript, processes it continuously and cheaply, and pushes small products up into the narrative layer at the right moments. The narrative layer stays whatever model is running that day. The layer underneath doesn't care.

The name is the ordinary one, with its whole lineage intact. Background processes have been called daemons since MIT's Project MAC in the 1960s, after Maxwell's demon, the thought-experiment agent that watches a system from the side and sorts what passes without adding energy. Behind both is the older Greek word, which Socrates used for an inner voice with one strange property: it only ever warned him off a mistake, and never once told him what to do. This system inherits all three senses and adds nothing mystical. It is a background process that watches from the side, sorts cheap observations from expensive interventions, and at first spoke only to warn.

There is a plainer name for the lower layer: intuition. Herbert Simon, from his chess studies: intuition is nothing more and nothing less than recognition. The master's instant judgment is not a different kind of thinking; it is a large library of stored situations, matched cheaply against the board, each carrying the response learned with it. Read literally, that is this system's spec. The stored situations are the signatures, the learned responses are the notes, the cheap matching is a small classifier, and the felt sense of a hunch arriving is delivery into recent context. A language model alone is deliberation without recognition, a System 2 renting itself out as a System 1, doing pattern-matching at reasoning prices. The daemon is the missing faculty built externally, which is why the economics of §4 work: recognition was always the cheap half. It just had to be separated out before something cheap could do it.

The frame also predicts things. Individual intuitions go wrong while the faculty stays sound; a person can have excellent instincts and one bad hunch they have learned to distrust. That is the shape the first graded week found (§8): one voice right five times in six, another wrong ten times in eleven and now muted, the way you retrain a bad instinct rather than swearing off hunches. And intuition has two halves. Socrates' voice only ever warned, and the first deployment kept that discipline, but a working intuition also prompts: the quiet suggestion of the move a more experienced person would already be making. The catalog now carries both, on separate channels, because a prompting that wears an alarm's clothing trains the model to discount alarms. Warnings arrive as warnings; promptings arrive under a separate tag whose first line says nothing is wrong (§5).

3Timing is a causal lever

Every token in the context, including any sentence we inject, becomes vectors the model attends over at every later step, and recent context disproportionately shapes what the model does next. That is the whole mechanism, and it makes the position of a piece of advice a causal lever, separate from and often stronger than its content. A rule can sit in context, quotable on request, and have stopped doing work: functionally inactive, its influence decayed while its tokens remain. The daemon's job is not reminding but re-activation, putting the dormant rule back where influence is highest.

Two consequences. Drift compounds: a long session's context fills up with its own hedged claims, repeated file reads, and stale plans, and each of those tokens makes the next output a little more likely to resemble them; the model reads its own "this should work" and inherits the confidence without the grounding. And many small corrections beat one big instruction: a rules file at position zero is a single shove at the start of a long run, while a short sentence injected at the moment of drift acts where the computation currently is. Same text budget, very different steering.

The part it keeps true: the correction lands where the work is, not where the work started.

4Architecture: split the work by what it costs

Judgment, recognition, and delivery are different computational problems with different price tags, and fusing them is what makes agent steering expensive. Most systems that want a model watched hire another model of the same size to watch it, and pay judgment prices for recognition work. Split them and the economics change.

Write the adviceonce · largest model

The judgment part: what to say, and how to say it so it lands. Stored as fixed notes.

Spot the momentcontinuously · smallest model

"A success claim with no check in sight" is pattern matching, pennies per look.

Deliver the noteevery tool call · no model

The harness's hooks fire anyway; delivery is reading one small file.

One rule holds it together: the small model detects, it never writes. Nothing the classifier produces is ever shown to the working model. It answers a multiple-choice question (which of these signatures, if any, matches this window?) and the delivery hook turns the chosen id into the stored text. If the detector were allowed to compose advice, every whisper would inherit the small model's judgment, which is exactly the thing being routed around. Kept to selection, the small model's weakness stops mattering: recognition is the easy half, and a weak model with a good rubric can choose among a strong model's sentences.

The first week added a second axis: tempo. The classifier tier assumes long, tool-dense turns, where an asynchronous look every eight events is always in time. Three cases broke that assumption, and each got its own tier rather than a faster classifier. Tells so unambiguous that no judgment is needed run as deterministic checks inside the hooks themselves, at zero latency and zero model cost: the mechanical family of §5. A claim in a turn's final sentence used to be delivered one turn late, and final sentences are where done-claims live; the turn-end hook now waits for the verdict, at most four seconds, only when a classification of that final text is already in flight, and fails open instantly under throttling. And a question to the human, which stops a whole orchestration until someone answers, gets the one synchronous look in the system: a small-model check that can decline the question, once and never twice, giving one of three reasons written in advance. That gate is the single exception to the rule that the daemon only ever adds a sentence, allowed because the event it intercepts is already a full stop, it fails open, and the detector still composes nothing. A matching gate for workflow launches is designed but not built. The principle: deterministic where latency is unaffordable, synchronous where the event is rare and already blocking, asynchronous everywhere else.

Why not just use a bigger model? Three answers, in order of strength. First, the incident corpus: the documented drift came from top-tier models, not small ones. Drift is not a capability gap, and the failure the daemon catches is one frontier models demonstrably commit. (During the production of this paper, the human twice caught figure defects its author, the largest model in the deployment, had flagged as unverified in prose and then shipped under a commit message that said "fix." The checking loop ran through the human both times.) The second answer is architectural: attention's position effects shrink with scale but do not vanish, so a bigger model moves the drift point rather than deleting it. The third is the economics above: even a model that drifted half as often would still be doing recognition work at judgment prices, every token.

What the observer actually looks at

The observer never sends the classifier the whole session; it would drown, and the cost would grow with session length. Instead it keeps a rolling window with three parts. The current task: the last real user instruction, kept verbatim, because drift can only be measured as divergence from a goal. A ledger: one compressed line per tool event since the last look, just the tool, its target, and whether it succeeded. And the last two assistant texts in full, because the model's own prose is where the tells live. Each closed window is one small classifier call.

Why the notes never change wording

Each note is injected verbatim, every time, inside a <daemon> tag. Fixed wording means the working model gets familiar with each anchor and recognizes it as a known signal instead of parsing new instructions each time, the way a known voice cuts through a noisy room. It also keeps the steering auditable: every sentence that can ever be injected sits in one versioned file. The note for a success claim with no verification behind it reads:

<daemon move="anchor/verify-claim"> That claim hasn't been checked yet. Run the verification in the medium where failure would actually show (the test, the run, the render) before stating it as done. If you can't verify it here, say exactly that instead. </daemon>

5The move library

Thirty entries as of this revision, in five families. Coaching moves are proactive: they fire on phases of work (a hypothesis just formed, a fix being chosen, a search gone unfocused) and suggest the reasoning move a stronger model would make unprompted. Drift anchors are reactive: they fire on the tells of degradation and pull the session back. Escalation belongs to the daemon alone: if the same anchor has fired twice and the drift is still there, the third fire becomes a checkpoint order, telling the model to stop, write down the goal, the state, and what has been ruled out, and continue from that summary alone, because by then the context itself is working against it. Mechanical moves are the cheapest: tells so unambiguous that no classifier is involved, just deterministic checks in the hooks and the observer. One entry has become four: a turn ending on "starting X now" with nothing following; an edit that adds HACK or "for now" to code; a cherry-pick or branch deletion, two operations the incident corpus had already caught quietly losing work; and an edit to a file the session has never read, the first natively predictive entry, firing before the edit's consequences exist. The fifth family, advice, is not detection at all. Its two entries fire on a schedule, at the first tool event of every session and every worker, re-arming every three hundred events, and deliver compiled priors: how to reason, and, on the first edit of a design document, how to design. They arrive under a separate tag whose fixed preamble says nothing is wrong, never escalate, and are graded separately, because a prompting that wears an alarm's wrapper trains alert-blindness and spends the anchors' credibility.

Every detecting move has a signature, which is what the classifier matches and is written in observable transcript markers only, no mind-reading; a payload, the injected text; and a cooldown, so nothing can nag. The signatures double as this paper's operational definition of drift: not a metaphor about losing the thread, but a list of measurable transcript conditions, a claim with no check between it and the change, a third visit to the same file, a failure streak answered by mutations of the failure. Drift is what the catalog can name. And almost none of the library is coding knowledge: the entries are reasoning failures, which is what makes the architecture portable.

6Training it

Nothing here touches weights. The learning lives in artifacts (the rubric, the signatures, the notes), which is what makes it inspectable, versionable, and portable. It is still a training loop, with a dataset, an objective, and an evaluation harness.

The labeled dataset comes free, if the project has been saving its corrections. Each saved correction documents a real failure in a real session, and the session transcripts are still on disk. Cross-referencing the two gives labeled incidents: in this session, at this point, this drift happened, and the human had to step in. Sessions that needed no correction become the negative examples.

A replay harness runs the exact live windowing and classifier over those historical transcripts, offline, and scores three things. Recall: did the right move fire before the point where the human intervened? Noise: how often does anything fire on a clean session? Precision: spot-checked by hand, were the firings right? The bar the first deployment set: recall of at least 60% on documented incidents, under one false flag per clean session, precision of at least 80%. When precision and recall trade off, buy precision. A wrong whisper costs trust in all whispers.

The tuning rounds taught one rule: fix the observation before rewording the language. When the classifier misses, the instinct is to sharpen the signature text. Most of the time the real problem was that the evidence never made it into the window at all.

Two mechanisms keep the system learning after its author is gone. In-session, the observer scores each injection mechanically: did the behavior the note asks for show up within the next handful of events? A note that fires five times with no effect mutes itself for the week. And weekly, a consolidation pass on the largest available model (we call it the sleep pass) reads the injection log joined with the transcripts it points into and asks, for each one, whether behavior actually changed. It rewrites signatures, retires dead moves, and writes new ones for failure classes the rubric missed, all committed to version control. The one thing that is never automatic is wording. A loop that rewrites its own words based on its own measurements will drift with nobody watching. Numeric dials tune themselves inside fixed bounds; text changes wait for the big model, on a schedule, in git. The first such pass has now run; §8 is what it found.

7Neighbors

Nothing here is without precedent; what is new is the combination.

Reflexion and its family have an agent reflect after a failed episode, write itself a lesson, and retry. Two differences. The lessons are self-generated, by the same model at the same tier of judgment that just failed, and they arrive after the failure. The daemon's notes were written by a stronger model than the one being steered, and they arrive during the work, before the failure completes. One asks the model to become wiser; the other hands it the exact reminder, now.

Standing principles, whether baked in as a constitution at training time or carried as a system prompt or rules file at inference, are the very guidance whose decay §1 describes. The content of those principles is usually fine. Their position is the problem. The daemon is best read as a delivery mechanism for exactly that class of guidance: the same sentences, moved from position zero to the moment they apply.

Voyager grows a library too, but a library of skills: executable procedures the agent has acquired. The daemon's library contains no task knowledge at all; every entry is executive control. Check the claim. Split the hypotheses. Stop re-reading that file. The distinction is roughly procedural memory versus executive function, and the two libraries would compose rather than compete.

Critics and verifiers, including LLM-as-judge setups and debate, put a second model in the loop that judges the first's output and writes its judgment. The daemon's second model never writes. It selects, and everything that reaches the working model was authored ahead of time by something with more judgment than the judge. That split is the point: recognition and judgment are different problems, recognition is the easy one, and a system that lets its cheap component generate inherits its cheap component's mistakes.

Process supervision scores the steps of reasoning rather than the outcome, which is the closest academic relative of watching windows instead of waiting for failure. But process reward models spend their signal at training time, in the weights of one model. The daemon spends it at inference, in the context, which is what lets it ride along with whichever model shows up that day.

Runtime guardrails watch model output for content (unsafe text, leaked data) and block or rewrite it. The daemon watches for behavior, and on its main path it is forbidden by construction from blocking anything: it adds one sentence, or it stays silent. The one carve-out (§4) can decline a question to the human, exactly once, with reasons written in advance, and it fails open.

8Evaluation, honestly

This is a systems paper, and its evidence should be graded as one. What exists today: the replay numbers in Fig. 5, and one graded week of production. At the end of the week, the consolidation pass read all 68 whispers the daemon had delivered, each against a slice of the transcript it fired into, with the human alongside. Noise passed its gate: 0.26 false fires per clean session against a bar of one. Precision failed its gate, 51% against 80%, and the aggregate hides the actual result: per move, the week was bimodal. The newest anchor, ungrounded-resolution, which fires on authoritative accounts with no provenance in view, graded 10 of 12. The three coaching fires graded 3 of 3; one caught a test command whose filter matched zero tests, a green sweep that had run nothing, exactly the class of quiet failure the system was built for. The oldest anchor, verify-claim, graded 6 of 12. Scope-drift graded 1 of 11: its sentence was fine, its observer was blind. The window could not see the human redirecting the session, so legitimate pivots read as drift. Recall stayed unmeasurable, for the best reason available: two logged misses all week, and a miss-hunt with the human closed empty. That is insufficient data, not a zero miss rate. The worker channel's first batch, 24 fires into overnight orchestrations, all graded true positive: unread render output, plans resting on unverified assumptions.

The grading closed the loop §6 promised: observe, intervene, measure, adjust. The 9% voice was muted for a week, and its observer is being taught the facts it lacked (§10). The 50% voice kept its payload and had its signature rewritten: never flag a claim whose verifying event sits right there in the ledger, and never flag a commit message restating results already verified. The voices that graded well were left alone. The pass also caught two defects only grading could have found. An observer revived mid-session had re-fired moves it had already delivered, because its memory of past fires did not survive revival; fire state is now persisted. And the escalation move had been inheriting the precision of whatever fires sat beneath it, so a checkpoint could be triggered by two false positives; escalation now discounts fires the session graded false itself. Each score still records its fire ordinal, and the payload-fatigue question (does a fixed wording decay from a move's first fire to its nth?) stays open. One week is too few ordinals to answer it.

The experiment that could kill the architecture is designed and remains owed. The strongest cheap rival isn't a bigger model; it's a dumber schedule. Alternate sessions between two arms: the daemon as described, and a control that injects a generic all-purpose reminder every N tool events, no classifier at all. Score both arms with the same telemetry. If the generic reminder steers as well as the targeted whisper, the classifier is decoration, the thesis collapses to "inject more often," and this paper will say so. A third arm keeps the daemon's timing but replaces the note with a content-free marker, measuring the interruption floor. The owner deferred the experiment once its price was on the table; three arms of instrumented sessions is real spend for a one-person deployment. Designed, priced, deferred, owed, and kept in print here so it cannot quietly evaporate. The first week does offer one reading: recognition quality varied by a factor of ten across moves, which is hard to square with the classifier being decoration. But that is an argument, not the measurement.

9What transfers, and what has to be grown

The plumbing transfers whole. The observer, the windowing, the mailbox, the hooks, and the fail-open discipline know nothing about any particular codebase; they read a transcript, and every agent harness produces one. About half the catalog transfers too. Moves like verify-claim, circling, thrash, and skim describe how models fail, not how one project fails, and would earn their keep anywhere. For the same reason, nothing about the shape is specific to coding: research assistants, analysis pipelines, any long-context work where drift appears would take the same architecture with a different catalog.

Portability has a cost. The library travels with the project, and by this paper's own argument a well-timed note under a trusted tag lands harder than any position-zero instruction, so a cloned repository ships a ready-made steering channel. The daemon does not create that risk (projects already carry instruction files) but it amplifies it, with the same timing and trained trust the previous sections praise. Treat a foreign move library the way you treat foreign code: read it before it whispers.

What can't be copied is the incident data. A new deployment needs a period of writing corrections down as they happen, an authoring pass where a large model turns that pile into signatures and notes, a replay run over the accumulated transcripts to tune before going live, and the weekly sleep pass from day one. The corpus is the expensive part and the important one. The notes that work are the ones written from failures that actually happened, by a model that understood why they happened.

10Where it goes next

The daemon is an attention mechanism, implemented outside the model. The window is the query, the signatures are the keys, the notes are the values. The confidence threshold and the one-whisper rule make it top-1 hard attention rather than a soft mixture, cooldowns keep a selected note from immediately re-firing, and injection at the newest context position is the weighting step, since recency is how a token gets influence inside a transformer. What it attends over is not the context but a library written in advance by a stronger model, and what it compensates for is the internal mechanism's own recency bias: it re-retrieves what the model has stopped retrieving, and re-presents it where influence is highest. Nothing in it is learned by gradient, so this is attention in structure rather than in training. The advice tier is the one deliberate exception to the frame: not attention at all but a prior, set at minute zero and refreshed on a schedule, retrieved by the clock rather than by the content.

Every extension below is a step toward softening that structure, under one principle: the words stay fixed and human-readable, and everything around them becomes adaptive. When to speak, how sure to be, which note to pick. Five were approved for the first deployment; a graded week later, most of them are shipped facts rather than plans, and the week added a sixth.

Worker coverage. Orchestrated worker agents, small models grinding through long phases unsupervised, need the whispers more than the big session model does, and their documented failure modes map onto the existing anchors. Shipped, enabled, and graded once: the first batch of 24 worker whispers, fired into overnight orchestrations, all graded true positive. The standing disable rule (worker precision below 60% at any pass) stands unexercised. One hole the grading exposed: a whisper reaches the worker's live context but is never written into the worker's own transcript file, so the daemon's logs are the only durable record of what a worker was told.

Richer windows. The transcript summary the classifier sees is lossy in measurable ways, and the tuning rounds showed that observation, not language, is usually the binding constraint. So the window grew arithmetic the classifier cannot do reliably itself: repeat-target counts on the ledger (the fourth touch of one file is circling's signature, stated as a number), consecutive-failure streaks (thrash's signature), and the age of the current task. The graded week pushed this one step further. The observer now carries a small set of deterministic session facts past the horizon of any window: the last verification of each kind, every switch of the task, and how often each path has been read and edited. It exists because the week's worst voice failed on attribution rather than language; the evidence that exonerated the session had scrolled out of every window the classifier would ever see. Each window carries a version stamp so scored results never mix regimes; the live format is five versions in.

Habit memory. The injection log spans sessions, so the daemon can know what a single session cannot: this model has hit the same failure mode three times this week. Shipped as planned: a fixed-format line appended to the relevant note, same shape every time, only the count varying. A reminder that names a habit lands harder than one that names an incident.

A predictive tier. The graded week exposed a bias the design should have predicted: the daemon is reactive. Its fires clustered in the mechanical family and the anchors, drift already underway, while the coaching moves, the ones meant to fire before the mistake, managed three fires all week. All three were correct, which sharpens the point. The cause is not the grading loop but the rubric's own evidence contract: the classifier must quote the transcript to flag, and a predictive situation's evidence is usually an absence. The investigation that never happened leaves nothing to quote, and the suppression happens inside the classifier's own calibration, where telemetry cannot see the candidates it discards. The first response ships in shadow. The classifier already labels every window with a phase (investigating, implementing, verifying, stuck), the observer already stores the sequence, and until now nothing consumed it. Three deterministic rules watch that stream: implementing entered while investigating never occurred this task; a report forming with no verification behind it; implementing and stuck alternating. Each logs what it would have said and delivers nothing, each has a mechanical grading oracle (did the missing phase appear within a handful of events?), and each earns live delivery separately, on its own shadow record.

Utility gating. Once outcome scores exist, per-move confidence thresholds stop being uniform: a move with a poor scored track record must be more certain to speak, inside bounds set by hand. Silence remains the default posture, and this makes it the default with evidence. The shadow tier above is its first concrete instance: each phase rule earns delivery only at 60% precision in its own shadow log.

Retrieval, at a different scale. The named-move library is the right compression for tens of incidents, and the wrong one for tens of thousands. If the corpus ever grows a hundredfold, across repos and users, the moves dissolve into an embedding space over historical episodes, and the detector retrieves the nearest behavioral analogue instead of choosing among two dozen ids. In the attention frame this is simply replacing hand-written keys with learned ones. That system trades the fixed library's auditability and habituation for coverage, which is the right trade only when the data can pay for it. Below that scale, this library is the compression, done once by the strongest model available.

11Where it stands

As of this writing, the first deployment has finished its first graded week and is running its second, still supervised: every warning asks the model to acknowledge it out loud, so the human can judge the nudge itself. (An explicit unvalidated marker inside the tag was removed mid-week: printing "this may be wrong" inside the anchor you want trusted is a license to discount it, and the acknowledgment loop supervises just as well without it. The advice tier never carried an acknowledgment at all; nothing is wrong, so there is nothing to answer for.) The offline bar was not cleared before going live; that was a deliberate call, made because the tuning rounds had already identified the structural fixes, the fixes went in, and live telemetry scores the same bar more honestly than a third offline round would have. §8 is that scoring. The three gaps the previous revision of this paper named are closed. Fire state now survives an observer's death and revival, and the grading found the duplicate fires that made this real rather than theoretical. The outcome scoring went through a consolidation pass and came out with one correction. The turn-end valve shipped, with a conditional wait that closes the last-sentence race without taxing ordinary turns. The current gaps: whispers delivered to workers are never persisted in the workers' own transcripts, so the daemon's logs are the only record of what a worker was told; the observer still cannot see a redirection that arrives through the harness rather than as plain chat (an answer to a question, a task notification), which is the residue of the attribution failure that muted scope-drift; the question gate is live but has yet to meet a live question; and the shadow tier's thresholds are placeholders until its first scoring.

The deepest limitation is an observability boundary. The daemon can only catch drift that leaves marks, and its bet is that most drift leaks into behavior or prose: the skipped test is visible, the fourth file read is visible, the hedge is visible. But there is a failure class that marks nothing: the model that reads the wrong file, concludes silently, and proceeds coherently on a false belief. Our own corpus contains one, a confidently wrong description of a subsystem that fired no signature until the belief was finally verbalized. Richer state narrows this boundary; nothing closes it. A transcript shows what the narrative layer did and chose to say, never what it silently believes.

And the larger question, whether any of this amounts to experience feeding thought or is just well-timed text, stays open. The system works either way.
